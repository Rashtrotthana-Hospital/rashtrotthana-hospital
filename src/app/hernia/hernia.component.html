What is a Hernia?

A hernia occurs when an internal organ or fatty tissue pushes through a weak spot in the surrounding muscle or connective tissue - causing pain, discomfort and visible bulging, especially during physical activity, coughing or prolonged standing.

!

If you notice a bulge in your abdomen, groin or chest - especially one that worsens with activity or causes persistent pain - consult a general surgeon at the earliest to prevent complications.

Types of Hernia We Treat

Our general surgeons are experienced in diagnosing and treating all major types of hernias - from common to complex cases.

Most Common

Inguinal Hernia

Occurs when tissue (often part of the intestine) pushes through a weak spot in the abdominal muscles into the inguinal canal in the groin. More prevalent in men.

  • Groin bulge, especially when bending
  • Pain or discomfort in the groin
  • Treatable with laparoscopic repair

Umbilical Hernia

Fatty tissue or part of the bowel pushes through the abdominal wall near the navel. Common in infants and overweight adults and typically painless initially.

  • Soft bulge near the belly button
  • Often visible when straining
  • Day-care laparoscopic procedure

Hiatal Hernia

The upper part of the stomach bulges through the diaphragm into the chest cavity. Often associated with acid reflux and heartburn symptoms.

  • Heartburn and acid reflux
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Managed medically or surgically

Incisional Hernia

Develops at the site of a previous abdominal surgery where the muscles have weakened. Tissue pushes through the scar tissue at or near the surgical incision.

  • Bulge at or near a surgical scar
  • Discomfort during activity
  • Requires mesh-reinforced repair

Epigastric Hernia

Fat pushes through a weakness in the abdominal wall between the navel and lower breastbone (epigastric region). Often small but can cause persistent discomfort.

  • Small lump in upper abdomen
  • Pain when pressing the area
  • Surgically corrected with mesh
