What Is Vascular Disease?

Vascular disease refers to any condition affecting your blood vessels - arteries, veins, capillaries - and the lymphatic system. These conditions can disrupt blood flow, lead to complications like strokes or limb loss, and reduce your quality of life.

Conditions We Commonly Treat:

  • Peripheral artery disease (PAD)
  • Carotid artery narrowing
  • Aortic aneurysms
  • Varicose and spider veins
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
  • Chronic venous insufficiency
  • Diabetic foot and vascular wounds
  • Lymphatic disorders

What Are the Types of Vascular Surgery?

Vascular surgery includes a wide range of procedures - from minimally invasive techniques to complex open surgeries - aimed at restoring healthy blood flow and preventing complications. Common types of vascular surgeries include:

Aneurysm Repair

  • Open Aneurysm Surgery: Involves making a large incision to replace the weakened section of the aorta with a synthetic graft.
  • Endovascular Aneurysm Repair (EVAR): A less invasive approach using a stent graft to reinforce the artery wall through small incisions.

Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD) Treatments

  • Angioplasty and Stenting: Involves inserting a balloon and stent to widen narrowed arteries and improve circulation
  • Atherectomy: A catheter-based technique that removes plaque from inside the arteries.
  • Bypass Surgery: Creates a new pathway around blocked arteries using a graft to reroute blood flow.

Carotid Artery Procedures

  • Carotid Endarterectomy: Surgical removal of plaque from the carotid arteries to reduce the risk of stroke.
  • Carotid Angioplasty with Stenting: A minimally invasive method to open blocked carotid arteries using a stent.

Venous Procedures

  • Varicose Vein Removal: Done using vein stripping or micro-incisions (phlebectomy) to remove faulty veins.
  • Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA): Uses thermal energy to seal problematic veins and redirect blood flow.

Dialysis Access Surgery

Creates arteriovenous (AV) fistulas or grafts to ensure reliable access for hemodialysis in patients with advanced kidney disease.

Are There Risks Associated with Vascular Surgery?

As with any surgical procedure, vascular surgery carries potential risks, which may include:

  • Bleeding or bruising
  • Infection
  • Blood clots
  • Damage to nearby tissues or nerves
  • Reaction to anesthesia
  • Rare but serious complications like stroke or heart attack

These risks vary depending on the patient’s health, the type of procedure, and the complexity of the condition. However, with proper pre-operative evaluation and skilled surgical care, complications are minimized.

How Effective Is Vascular Surgery?

Vascular surgery is often highly effective in restoring circulation and preventing life-threatening events. Success rates are particularly strong in:

  • Preventing rupture of aneurysms
  • Lowering stroke risk from carotid artery disease
  • Relieving leg pain and improving walking distance in PAD patients
  • Reducing symptoms and cosmetic issues in venous disorders

Long-term outcomes also depend on adopting a healthy lifestyle and following follow-up care guidelines after surgery.
